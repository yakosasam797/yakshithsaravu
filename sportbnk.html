Background

Sportbnk is an AI-powered sponsorship intelligence platform designed to bridge the gap between brands, rights holders, and agencies in the sports sponsorship ecosystem. The industry relies on fragmented data, manual outreach, and disconnected tools — this project explores how thoughtful product design and information architecture can turn that complexity into clarity.

I was brought in to redesign existing screens. Instead, I took ownership of the product direction — defining the MVP scope, leading research, structuring the information architecture, and making the key product decisions that shaped what Sportbnk became. The design work followed from those decisions, not the other way around.

02 — Research approach

Understanding the problem before solving it

Before opening Figma, I spent time understanding the business, the market, and the people who would use the product. This was not a checkbox exercise — the research fundamentally changed what we built.

User interviews

Spoke with people from all three user types — brand marketers, rights holders (clubs), and agency partnership leads. Focused on understanding their daily workflows, not validating assumptions.

Founder strategy sessions

Multiple sessions with the founder to understand the business model, revenue strategy, market positioning, and what investors needed to see. This shaped the MVP scope directly.

Competitor analysis

Analysed four tools in the sponsorship space — SponsorUnited, Relo Metrics, KORE Software, OpenSponsorship — to identify what exists, what's missing, and where the market gap is.

Market landscape review

Studied how sponsorship deals actually work — the 6-18 month timelines, the role of agencies (15-20% of deal value), and where the knowledge gaps exist that Sportbnk could fill.

Key patterns that emerged

Three patterns kept appearing across every interview and analysis. These became the foundation of every product decision.

01

Sequential decision-making

Users don't make sponsorship decisions all at once. They discover, evaluate, then act — in that order. The product needed to mirror this flow, not fight it.

02

Fragmented data & tools

Everyone was working across spreadsheets, emails, and disconnected databases. No single tool covered the full workflow from discovery to deal management.

03

Timing is everything

The biggest pain wasn't finding partners — it was knowing who was ready right now. Budget cycles, contract windows, and competitive timing made "when" more important than "who."

How this changed the product direction

The original brief was to redesign screens. After the research, the problem was clear: the product didn't need better visuals — it needed a different structure. The platform had to be organised around user decision flow (discover → evaluate → act), not around data categories. That insight reshaped the entire information architecture.

I spoke with people from all three user types — brand marketers, commercial directors at clubs, and agency leads. I was not validating assumptions. I wanted to understand what their day actually looks like, where things break down, and what would make them switch from spreadsheets to a platform.

04 — Competitive research

The market gap that defined Sportbnk's position

I analysed four tools in this space. The consistent finding: every tool focuses on past data — what deals already happened, what ROI was achieved. None of them support forward-looking decisions or a complete workflow from discovery to deal management. That gap became Sportbnk's positioning.

Tool What it does What it doesn't do
Tracks sponsorship deals and has a large database of clubs and brands No signals, no CRM, mostly US-focused, only shows historical data
Measures ROI on deals that have already happened Only works after a deal is signed — no value before the deal
CRM for clubs and leagues to manage their sponsor relationships Built only for rights holders — brands and agencies cannot use it
Marketplace connecting brands with athletes Athletes only, completely different market from what Sportbnk is doing
Discovery + signals + contact unlock + CRM in one place The only tool that combines all of this for all three user types globally
Sportbnk's market position

Every existing tool only looks backward — it shows you what already happened. Sportbnk is the only platform that combines discovery, real-time signals, contact unlocking, and CRM into one system — and the only one that serves all three user types (brands, rights holders, agencies) globally. The product position: look forward, not backward.

05 — MVP strategy

What we built, what we didn't — and why

The hardest part of defining an MVP is not deciding what to build — it's deciding what to leave out. I worked with the founder to scope a product that was small enough to build fast but complete enough to tell a coherent story to investors and early users.

What was intentionally not built

Every feature below was discussed and deliberately excluded. Not because they weren't valuable, but because including them would have diluted focus and delayed launch.

HubSpot / CRM integration Users need to see value in Sportbnk's own CRM first. Integrating with external tools before proving the core experience would add complexity without clarity.
Bulk export Encourages users to take data out of the platform. The product's value is in keeping users inside the system, not giving them a spreadsheet to leave with.
Advanced filtering & saved searches Adds power-user functionality before we have power users. The MVP needs to prove the core loop works — discover, evaluate, act.
AI-powered recommendations Requires training data from user behaviour that doesn't exist yet. Promising AI before having data would set wrong expectations.
Multi-language support The initial users are English-speaking markets. Localisation effort should follow market expansion, not precede it.
The MVP philosophy

A product with five things that work well is more credible to a first user than one with fifteen things that feel half-finished. Every feature we kept had to earn its place by directly serving the core user journey: discover an opportunity, evaluate it, and act on it.

Business model × product design

The credit gate is a product decision, not a monetisation afterthought

The platform's revenue model is credit-based: browsing is free, but unlocking a contact's details costs one credit. I placed the credit gate at the People tab — the exact moment a user has already decided they want to take action. By that point, they've browsed organisations, reviewed the sponsor landscape, and identified the right person. Intent is at its highest. Asking for a credit there feels fair because the user already sees the value. Everything before that point is free to build trust and get users invested.

06 — Revenue model in the product

The credit gate is not a paywall — it's a design decision about where monetisation creates the least friction and the most perceived value. The entire Browse → Profile → People → Unlock flow was designed to build user conviction before asking for payment.

07 — Information architecture

Structure follows user intent, not data categories

The IA was designed around how users make decisions, not how data is organised internally. Each section in the navigation maps to a specific user need identified in research.

→
User need: "I need to know what to act on right now."
Top-level navigation, not a dashboard panel. This is the product's core differentiator — if it's buried, users don't feel the platform's value on first visit.
Organisations
→
User need: "I need to evaluate and then contact."
One page with two tabs (Sponsors + People) — designed to match the sequential intent of discover → evaluate → act. The credit gate sits at the transition from evaluation to action.
Companies

User need: "As a rights holder, I need to find brands — not be found."
Mirrors Organisations but for rights holders browsing brands. Same underlying data, different perspective. Rights holders are simultaneously using the product and being researched by it.
My Partners
→
User need: "I need to manage relationships, not just discover them."
The CRM layer that transforms Sportbnk from an occasional discovery tool to a daily-use platform. The more partners someone adds, the more relevant the signals become — creating a retention loop.
Dashboard
→
Design decision: Built last, not first.

08 — Key product decisions

Every decision had a reason — here are the most important ones

Each decision below follows the same structure: the problem that prompted it, what was decided, why, and what was traded off. These are the choices that defined the product.

Decision 01 MVP Scope
Problem

The founder wanted to include CRM integrations, advanced analytics, and several other features. The scope was growing beyond what could be built and tested quickly.

Decision

Kept the MVP deliberately small — five core features only — and documented every exclusion with reasoning.

Reasoning

A tight, coherent product builds more trust with early users and tells a clearer investor story than a feature-rich one that feels unfinished.

Trade-off: The founder wanted to show more to investors. I argued that fewer features, done well, demonstrate stronger product thinking than a long feature list.
Decision 02 Navigation
Problem

The original layout had Signals as a widget panel on the dashboard. Users would need to navigate to the dashboard first, then discover signals existed.

Decision

Moved Signals Feed to its own top-level navigation item — making it one of the first things users see.

Reasoning

Where something sits in the navigation tells users what the product thinks is important. Signals are the differentiator — they needed to be treated like one.

Trade-off: One more item in the navigation. Accepted because the signals screen is what makes Sportbnk different from a regular database.
Decision 03 Org Profile
Problem

Should Sponsors and People be separate screens or combined? Each has different content density and different user intent.

Decision

One organisation page with two tabs. Sponsors tab (free) and People tab (credit gate).

Reasoning

User intent is sequential — evaluate the organisation's sponsor landscape first, then decide whether to find a contact. That's one journey, not two.

Trade-off: The Sponsors tab is data-heavy; the People tab is simpler. That slight imbalance is worth the navigation simplicity.
Decision 04 Dashboard
Problem

The founder expected the Dashboard to be designed first — it's the "home page" and what investors would see in demos.

Decision

Designed the Dashboard last, after all other screens were complete.

Reasoning

A dashboard summarises a product. If you design it before understanding the product fully, you're summarising assumptions. To bridge the gap, I walked the founder through the Signals and Organisations flow first.

Trade-off: Delayed a screen the founder wanted early. The final dashboard ended up being stronger because it reflected real user journeys, not guesses.
Decision 05 User Types
Problem

Rights holders (clubs) are the hardest user type — they use the platform to find brand partners while simultaneously being browsed and evaluated by brands.

Decision

Built completely separate discovery flows for brands and rights holders, even though they share the same underlying data.

Reasoning

A club looking outward (finding brands) and a brand looking inward (evaluating a club) have different mental models. Forcing both into one view would confuse both.

Trade-off: More screens to design and maintain. Worth it because the alternative was a confusing experience that served neither user type well.
Decision 06 Retention
Problem

A pure discovery tool gets used occasionally — only when someone is looking for a new deal. Between searches, users have no reason to return.

Decision

Made My Partners (CRM) a core MVP feature, not a future add-on. Users can manage existing relationships inside Sportbnk.

Reasoning

The more partners someone adds, the more relevant the platform's signals become for them. This creates a retention loop — not just a one-time discovery tool.

Trade-off: Building a CRM layer added scope to the MVP. Accepted because without retention, the platform would never prove habitual use to investors.

Production screens from Figma

The main discovery view for brands. Filterable by sport, status, and deal type. The table columns follow the order a brand actually thinks: who is this org, what sport, who sponsors them already, are they worth pursuing.

Investor conversations

The Figma prototype and direction document were used directly in fundraising. The research and competitive positioning I did became the structure of the pitch narrative — not just "here's an app" but "here's why this product exists and how it works."

Product narrative

My work defined what Sportbnk is — not just how it looks. The positioning (forward-looking vs. backward-looking tools), the credit model rationale, and the IA decisions gave the founder a clear story to tell beyond the MVP.

Beta-ready product

The platform was ready for the first 10 users without any additional design work. The MVP scoping paid off — the product was focused enough to feel complete, and users had a clear path from discovery to action.

Direction document

I wrote a product direction document covering positioning, user types, IA rationale, and feature roadmap reasoning. This became a reference for engineering decisions and future product planning — extending the impact beyond the design itself.

The platform's job is to tell you what to do next — not just show you data.

11 — Looking forward

What could be explored next

No product is finished at its MVP. Here's what I would explore next based on what I learned during this project — and what I would do differently with more time.

Personalised signals

Currently, Signals show market-wide activity. With enough user data, signals could be weighted by the user's industry, past searches, and partner portfolio to surface the most relevant opportunities first.

AI-assisted deal matching

Once behavioural data exists, AI could suggest matches based on category fit, timing, and past deal patterns — but only when the training data is real, not assumed.

Onboarding optimisation

The MVP has no onboarding flow. With real user data, I'd design a guided first-run experience that gets users to their first "aha moment" — seeing a relevant signal or discovering a new potential partner.

What I'd do differently

With more time, I would have run a small usability test with the Figma prototype before handoff. The research informed the structure, but testing the actual flows with real users would have validated the IA decisions under real conditions.
